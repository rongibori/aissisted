Visual Language

Color is a signal,
not decoration.

Red is biology. Aqua is intelligence. White is the system that holds them together. Every visual decision laddered to those three roles.

Biology in motion

FINISH matte · never glossy
Deep, slightly desaturated, controlled. Use as moment color: caps, activation states, charts, hero punctuation. On brand-mark surfaces (where the wordmark IS the message), the red can carry a full surface — never inside product UI or reading environments.

Absence of noise

Soft Gold

Luxury whisper

Reserved for Layer 03 only. Cap rings, foil debossing, editorial accents. Used like a signature, not a decoration.

Always

  • +Lead with white. Use red as a signal — caps, activation moments, key data points, hero punctuation.
  • +Allow red to flood ONLY on brand-mark surfaces (where the wordmark IS the message): hero backdrops, brand mark plates, social brand frames.
  • +Pair red with cool counterweights — aqua, graphite, white. That triad is the visual signature.
  • +Maintain matte finishes everywhere. Physical and digital. Always.

Never

  • ×Bright, saturated, or fire-engine red. Never emergency-red palettes.
  • ×Red as a full background in product UI, reading environments, or long-form content.
  • ×Glossy gradients, holographic effects, or candy finishes.
  • ×Red as a warning state inside the product. Red is the brand — it cannot also be an alert.

Decision Filter

Before anything ships,
it must survive five questions.

This is the gate every piece of work passes through — designer, copywriter, founder. If a single answer is no, the work goes back. The filter is what keeps the brand from drifting under deadline pressure.

The Five Questions

  • 01
    Does it feel built for one person?
    Not a segment. Not an avatar. One human.
  • 02
    Is it simpler than what we'd be tempted to make?
    If you removed three more elements, would it still work? Then remove them.
  • 03
    Does the intelligence feel invisible in the lead?
    AI/intelligence belongs in the descriptor lockup ("(+ intelligent science)") and the credibility line. It never leads the rally cry. Three jobs, three registers.
  • 04
    Is the science felt, not just stated?
    A claim is a feature. A felt outcome is a benefit.
  • 05
    Could this only have come from Aissisted?
    Drop the logo. Could a competitor ship the same thing? Then it isn't ours.

Brand Drift Indicators

  • !1
    It uses the word "users" or "customers."
    We speak to a person. Always.
  • !2
    A bottle appears in the first frame of an ad.
    Lead with the state of being. The product is the resolution.
  • !3
    It feels like wellness. Or like biotech. Or like luxury alone.
    Aissisted is the merge. If it lives in one lane, it isn't us.
  • !4
    A claim has no underlying mechanism we can show.
    We earn trust through precision. Cut the claim or back it.
  • !5
    It would make a 25-year-old and a 65-year-old recoil for different reasons.
    The architecture exists so we can be precise. Use it.

Design Output Requirements

Every visual deliverable
answers six questions.

Before any concept moves to execution — packaging, hero film, ad, dashboard, deck — the brief must articulate all six. No exceptions. No "we'll figure it out in design." The clarity lives in the brief, not the pixels.

01

Visual concept description

One paragraph. Plain language. What the viewer sees in the first second, and what they understand in the second second. If you can't write it, you can't design it.

02

Color usage breakdown

Percentage allocation across white, graphite, red, aqua, gold. Red is a signal — it should rarely exceed 8% of the surface. Brand-mark exceptions called out explicitly.

03

Material & texture direction

Matte, frosted, brushed, soft-touch, paper-grain. Physical and digital. If a surface looks glossy or plastic, it isn't us. Specify the tactile language before any render starts.

04

Emotional intent

One sentence. What should the viewer feel — calm, certain, seen, curious, owned. The feeling drives composition, pacing, and silence. Without it, the work defaults to generic.

05

How personalization is expressed

Where does the work say "this is yours"? A name. A signature. A data line. A handwritten line. Personalization must be visible — not implied. If it's not on the surface, it isn't real.

06

How it reinforces "The Press"

Every asset connects back to the iconic moment — the cap, the activation line, the act of pressing. Even a banner ad echoes it through pacing, punctuation, or the swoosh of color. Nothing exists in isolation.

Final Filter · Five Benchmarks

If the answer to any of these is no,
the work is not ready.

A
Apple-level simplicity?
Could a child describe what it does? Is every removed element invisible because it was never needed?
N
Nike-level performance?
Does the work say you become more by using this — not just you get something?
Æ
Aesop-level design discipline?
Is every choice intentional, restrained, and quiet? Does silence do as much work as the typography?
B
Biologically real?
Does the science feel earned — referenced through mechanism, not adjective? No miracles. No magic.
★
Made for one person?
Strip every brand mark off. Could one human still recognize this as theirs? If yes, it ships.
If all five answer yes — and the six output requirements are documented — the work is on-brand. Then ship.
